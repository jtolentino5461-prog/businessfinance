Resources & Legal
California-specific legal and business resources for your clothing brand
Always consult a licensed California attorney or CPA before filing legal entity paperwork. Laws change frequently. Links are provided as starting points only.
🏢
LLC (Limited Liability Company) — California
Most popular structure for small clothing brands. Protects personal assets and is flexible for taxes.
  • Choose a unique business name and check availability on the CA Secretary of State website (bizfileonline.sos.ca.gov)
  • File Articles of Organization (Form LLC-1) with the CA Secretary of State — $70 filing fee
  • Designate a Registered Agent in California (can be yourself or a service)
  • Draft an Operating Agreement (not required by CA law, but strongly recommended for multi-member LLCs)
  • Obtain an EIN (Employer Identification Number) from the IRS — free at irs.gov
  • Register with the CA Franchise Tax Board and pay the $800 annual minimum franchise tax (due April 15)
  • File Statement of Information (Form LLC-12) within 90 days of formation, then every 2 years — $20 fee
  • Open a dedicated business bank account — never mix personal and business funds
  • Obtain any required local business licenses (city/county level varies)

S-Corporation Election
Elect S-Corp tax status to potentially reduce self-employment taxes once your brand is profitable (generally $40K+ net profit).
  • First form a California Corporation (Form ARTS-GS, $100) or an LLC that elects corporate taxation
  • File IRS Form 2553 to elect S-Corp status — must be filed within 75 days of formation or by March 15 of the tax year
  • File CA Form 3560 (S-Corporation Election) with the CA Franchise Tax Board
  • Set a reasonable salary for yourself as owner-employee — must be market-rate, IRS will scrutinize if too low
  • Run payroll (quarterly 941 filings, state payroll taxes via EDD)
  • File Federal Form 1120-S and CA Form 100S annually
  • Issue Schedule K-1 to all shareholders for their share of income/loss
  • Note: California S-Corps pay a 1.5% net income tax (minimum $800) plus the $800 franchise tax — factor this in

Trademark Your Brand Name & Logo
Critical for clothing brands — protect your name, logo, and designs from competitors.
  • Conduct a trademark search on the USPTO TESS database to check availability
  • Determine your trademark class — clothing falls under Class 25 (International Classification)
  • File a trademark application at USPTO.gov — filing fees range from $250–$350 per class (TEAS Plus vs. Standard)
  • Respond to any Office Actions from the USPTO within 3 months (extendable)
  • Receive Notice of Publication — 30-day opposition period begins
  • Once approved, maintain the trademark — file Declaration of Use (Section 8) between years 5–6, then renew every 10 years
  • Consider a California State Trademark as well (cheaper, faster, but only covers CA)

California Seller's Permit & Sales Tax
Required if you sell clothing in California. You must collect and remit CA sales tax (currently 7.25% base + local).
  • Register for a Seller's Permit through CDTFA (California Department of Tax and Fee Administration) — free
  • Collect California sales tax on all taxable sales — most clothing is taxable (unlike some states)
  • File CDTFA sales tax returns quarterly or monthly depending on sales volume
  • If selling online to out-of-state customers, understand economic nexus rules — you may owe tax in other states above $100K sales or 200 transactions
  • Consider using TaxJar or Avalara to automate multi-state sales tax compliance

Clothing Brand Compliance — California Garment Law
California has specific laws governing garment manufacturing. If you manufacture locally, compliance is required.
  • If manufacturing garments in CA, obtain a Garment Manufacturing License from the CA Labor Commissioner
  • Comply with SB 62 (Garment Worker Protection Act) — all workers must be paid hourly, not per piece
  • Ensure all clothing has required RN (Registered Identification Number) labels or your company name on care labels (FTC requirement)
  • Follow FTC Care Labeling Rules — all garments must include care instructions
  • Register with CPSC if manufacturing children's clothing — strict safety testing required
  • If importing fabrics or products, understand U.S. Customs duties and use an import broker if needed
  • Consider product liability insurance — essential for clothing brands selling to the public

🛡️
Business Insurance for Clothing Brands
Protect your brand with the right coverage from day one.
  • General Liability Insurance — covers bodily injury, property damage, advertising injury ($1M per occurrence recommended)
  • Product Liability Insurance — covers claims that your clothing caused harm (often bundled with general liability)
  • Commercial Property Insurance — covers your inventory, equipment, and studio
  • Business Owner's Policy (BOP) — bundles general liability + property, often cheaper than buying separately
  • If hiring employees: Workers' Compensation Insurance is required by California law from day one
